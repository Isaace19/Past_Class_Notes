Catalog Course Description:

This course is for students majoring in engineering, mathematics, and physics. It is a calculus-based approach to topics in electricity and magnetism.  The course requires 3 hours of lecture and 3 hours of laboratory when offered on campus.  The online version of this course uses established physics applets for its online experiments.  The course materials including its Laboratory Manual are all available to students in the college online system.

Prerequisites:  MATH 1920

Co-requisites:  ENS 1510; however, it is preferred to have completed ENS 1510 before taking this course.

Textbook(s) and Other Course Materials:

University Physics, Revised Edition, by Harris Benson (Wiley) may be used as reference.  The course materials including its Laboratory Manual are all available in the College Online System.

TBR General Education Goals: Natural Sciences*:

I

Conduct an experiment, collect and analyze data, and interpret results in a laboratory setting.

II

Analyze, evaluate and test a scientific hypothesis.

III

Use basic scientific language and processes, and be able to distinguish between scientific and non-scientific explanations.

IV

Identify unifying principles and repeatable patterns in nature, the values of natural diversity, and apply them to problems or issues of a scientific nature.

V

Analyze and discuss the impact of scientific discovery on human thought and behavior.

Expected Student Learning Outcomes*:

Students will be able to

  1. Apply learned physics concepts to theoretical and practical situations. (I, II, & III)
  2. Apply learned physics concepts to estimate an unknown parameter in a given practical situation by using the physics principle(s) involved. (I, II, III, and V)
  3. Have an understanding of energy calculation to estimate energy cost in a given situation. (V)
  4. Calculate and analyze the resultant force of a group of point charges on a single charge. (I & III)
  5. Calculate the potential and P.E. of point charges and parallel-plates capacitors. (III & IV)
  6. Calculate the charge, voltage, and energy stored in capacitors. (III & IV)
  7. Apply Ohm’s Law to simple parallel and series circuit problems to calculate the current through, voltage across, and energy consumption associated with each element. (I, II, and III)
  8. Apply Kirchhoff's rules to multi-loop circuits to solve for unknown currents.  (III & IV)
  9. Solve RC circuit problems and explain the effect of the time-constant of such circuits on the speed of the charging processes.  (III & IV)
  10. Explain magnetism and its cause, and calculate the force exerted by a uniform magnetic field on a moving charge. (III & IV)
  11. Apply Gauss’ Law to find the electric field for symmetric charge distributions. (III & IV)
  12. Solve magnetic induction problems and apply Faraday's law to calculate the emf produced by an induced magnetic flux.  (III & IV)
  13. Solve problems involving capacitive & inductive reactances in AC circuits.  (III & IV)
  14. Solve simple RCL series circuit problems. (III & IV)
  15. Explain electromagnetic spectrum and the relation between wave speed, frequency, and wavelength. (I, III, & IV)
  16. Write down Maxwell’s equations and interpret the concept of each. (I, III, & IV)
  17. Have an understanding of the electromagnetic energy transport in space and the Poynting vector. (III & IV)
  18. Apply sophomore level mathematics in the derivation of electricity and magnetism formulae. (II, III, and IV)

  Evaluation:

  1. Tests (Theory Portion):  75% of the course grade

Theory Grade = 0.70 (Mean of 11 Online Tests) + 0.30 (Proctored Final Exam) **

  1. Experiments (Lab Portion):  25% of the course grade

Lab Grade = (the sum of reports grades) / (the number of the reports).  10 experiments are designed for the course.  Each experiment requires a report that must be typed and at least spell-checked. Procedures for a standard lab report is given under the Lab Section with the title "Lab Report Format." 

To avoid a ZERO Lab Grade, at least 6 reports must be turned in.  No late lab report will be accepted and there are No Lab Make-ups will be given when the course is offered on campus.

Course Grade = 0.75(Theory Grade) + 0.25(Lab Grade)

  1. Field Work:

An instructor who finds an opportunity for site visits or field work may give a maximum of 10% to this evaluation measure by adjusting the percentage in Part A.

  Grade Appeal

           A student may only appeal a final grade if one or more of the following conditions exist:

  • Error in the calculation of the grade
  • Substantial deviation from the syllabus
  • Clear violation of Pellissippi State Community College and/or the Tennessee Board of Regents policies.

Students have a right to a clear explanation (from the instructor or dean, as appropriate) of actions taken by the instructor, such as how a grade was calculated. An appeal will not be considered because of general dissatisfaction with a grade, penalty, or outcome of a course. Disagreement with the instructor's professional judgement of the quality of the student's work and performance is also not an admissible basis for an academic appeal.

The first step in any appeal is to discuss the situation with the instructor. If no resolution is reached, the student's next step is to consult the dean of the academic department, who may continue the informal process of discussion and/or may inform the student of the process for a formal appeal.
